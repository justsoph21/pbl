Palaces in Myanmar are the one of the iconic things of Myanmar. As the kings used to live in Myanmar before, their palaces still remain. We can't even imagine how magnificent the kings of the past were. Here is the some famous palaces.

Mya Nan San Kyaw Palace

Mya Nan San Kayw (Mandalay palace) is the last royal palace of the last king(Thibaw Min) of Burmese. It has been restored many times and has become one of the most popular attractions to visit in Myanmar. The Mandalay Palace was constructed from 1857 to 1859 when King Mindon Min chose Mandalay as the capital in 1857 after transported from Amarapura.Some interesting things to see at the Mandalay Palace include the palace walls on the moat with Mandalay Hill in the distance, the watch tower which offers a great view, and the small museum that houses some interesting items.

Khan Baw Za Tha Di Palace

Kanbawzathadi Palace is a palace located in Bago, Myanmar. The original palace was built for King Bayinnaung in 1556 and consisted of 76 apartments and halls. It was burned down in 1599. It was reconstructed in 1990 and finished in 1992. Kanbawzathadi Golden Palace in Bago is a reconstruction of the original Royal palace from the second half of the 16th century. The very ornate golden palace gives a good impression of the splendor and wealth of the second Burmese empire. It was rebuilt following the original design, based on knowledge gained from excavations and the original drawings of the building.

Shwebon Yadana Mingalar Palace

Shwebon Yadana Mingalar Palace is a royal palace located in Shwebo, Myanmar. It was originally built in 1753 AD by King Alaungphaya U Aung Zeya, who was the first founder of the Konbaung dynasty and was the capital city until 1760 when the capital was moved to nearby Sagain. The palace is centered on a large graduated pavilion and has two walls. The outer wall forms a 3.5 km square closure, surrounded by a moat. The tomb of King Alaungpaya is located to the southeast of the palace building. It was reconstructed in 1999 based on a mid-19th-century manuscript on a November 1853 expedition to collect the remaining timber posts of the palace, carve them out, and turn them into sacred images.
